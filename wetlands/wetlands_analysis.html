PARKS

California Protected Areas Database

CPAD includes large (e.g., national forests) and small (e.g., urban parks) open spaces, classified into 2x categories of public access: open and restricted (i.e., requires permit or has limited hours of operation, but still publicly accessible)

CPAD parks are represented at 3x geographic levels:

  • HOLDINGS: assessor parcels (smallest geospatial unit)
  • UNITS: holdings with the same name, within a single county
  • SUPER UNITS: holdings with the same name, manager, and access type, regardless of county (largest geospatial unit)

This CPAD unit taxonomy introduced certain challenges (e.g., all BLM holdings in the state, sharing the name “BLM,” were considered a single multipolygon at the super unit level). The steps I took to handle such challenges are detailed below.

WETLANDS

National Wetlands Inventory

The NWI maintains data on U.S. wetland and deepwater habitats. Importantly, the NWI uses “a biological definition of wetlands” that may not align with policy or scholarly classification of wetlands. The NWI Classification Scheme considers all marine and estuarine intertidal habitat to be wetland habitat, including beaches and rocky shores.

##### WETLANDS
wetlands_full <- st_read("data/wetlands/nwi_habitats.gdb", as_tibble = TRUE) %>%
  clean_names() %>%
  filter(wetland_type == "Estuarine and Marine Wetland") %>%
  mutate(wetland_type = case_when(
    grepl("^M2AB", attribute) ~ "marine (aquatic bed)",
    grepl("^M2RF", attribute) ~ "marine (reef)",
    grepl("^M2RS", attribute) ~ "marine (rocky shore)",
    grepl("^M2US", attribute) ~ "marine (unconsolidated shore)",
    grepl("^E2AB", attribute) ~ "estuarine (aquatic bed)",
    grepl("^E2RF", attribute) ~ "estuarine (reef)",
    grepl("^E2SB", attribute) ~ "estuarine (streambed)",
    grepl("^E2RS", attribute) ~ "estuarine (rocky shore)",
    grepl("^E2US", attribute) ~ "estuarine (unconsolidated shore)",
    grepl("^E2EM", attribute) ~ "estuarine (emergent)",
    grepl("^E2SS", attribute) ~ "estuarine (scrub-shrub)",
    grepl("^E2FO", attribute) ~ "estuarine (forested)"
  )) %>%
  select(-shape_length) %>%
  mutate(
    acres = round(set_units(acres, "acre"), 2),
    shape_area = round(set_units(shape_area, "m^2"), 2)
  ) %>%
  rename(wetland_acres = acres) %>%
  rowid_to_column("wetland_id")

##### SIMPLIFY FOR MAPS

    wetland_acres = sum(wetland_acres)
  ) %>%
  st_union(by_feature = TRUE) %>%
  st_cast("MULTIPOLYGON")

STEPS

1. crop and merge park holdings into superunits

I downloaded CPAD park polygons at the smallest geographic unit (holdings) and cropped to the Coastal Study Zone, prior dissolving them to a larger geographic unit (super units). Super units consolidate open land holdings that share a name, managing agency, and access level into a single multipolygon, effectively representing parks for recreational purposes (i.e., are closer to what people consider “parks” in their day-to-day activities).


I cropped and then merged holdings data, rather than starting with the pre-existing CPAD super units data, to exclude holdings that were significantly inland from the Coastal Study Zone that would otherwise be merged into coastal park polygons by virtue of the CPAD land taxonomy. This approach excluded inland BLM holdings across the state (all considered a single super unit), as well as inland portions of massive state parks.

##### CROP + MERGE PARK HOLDINGS

3. find parks intersecting wetlands

Within the Coastal Study Zone, three super units required additional wrangling before the full super units data could be intersected with the wetland polygons: the “BLM,” “California State Lands Commission,” and “City of Eureka lot” super units.

Multiple groups of holdings at different locations along the coast shared these names, and were thus consolidated into super units despite being decidedly distinct parks for recreational (vs. management) purposes.

For these three super units, I performed the wetland intersection at the holding level prior to merging them into super units, to exclude any holdings scattered across the California Coast that were not actually adjacent to wetlands. After performing the intersection at the holding level, there was a single, contiguous group of holdings intersecting wetlands for each super unit; there was thus no need to further differentiate between distinct groups of holdings within each super unit before merging the wetland holdings back to the super unit level.

##### FIND HOLDING EXCEPTIONS THAT INTERSECT WETLANDS

6. find regions

I classified wetland-parks into regions using the 4th California Climate Assessment regions. After meging the “San Diego” and “Los Angeles” regions, I assigned wetlands to one of four regions: North Coast, Bay Area, Central Coast, or South Coast.

7. find wetland acres

For each wetland-park polygon, I produced two metrics summarizing the size of wetland each park provides access to, in addition to the binary presence or absence of wetland intersecting a given park. I found (1) the sum total acres of wetland polygons that a given park intersects and (2) the cropped acres of wetland contained within the boundaries of the park.

##### FIND WETLAND ACRES
wetlandParks_acres <- wetlands %>%
  st_intersection(y = wetlandParks) %>%
